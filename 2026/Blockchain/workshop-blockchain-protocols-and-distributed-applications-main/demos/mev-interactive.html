01 / 07 — Concept

What is MEV?

Maximal Extractable Value (formerly "Miner Extractable Value") is the maximum profit that can be extracted by controlling the order of transactions within a block — beyond standard block rewards and gas fees.

🧱 Who Controls Transaction Order?

In a blockchain, a validator (or miner) decides which transactions to include in a block and in what order. Normally this is based on gas fees — highest gas = first in. But a validator can also reorder, insert, or exclude transactions to profit from price effects. Sophisticated bots (called "searchers") detect opportunities and pay high gas fees to validators to get their transactions placed optimally.

Imagine you're at a stock exchange in the 1980s — before electronic trading. You submit a buy order for 10,000 shares through a broker. The broker can see your order before executing it. They quickly buy shares for their own account first, driving the price up slightly — then fill your order at the higher price, then sell their shares. You got a slightly worse deal; they pocketed the difference. This is front-running — and blockchains make it even easier because every pending transaction is publicly visible.

🔍 Who Are the Searchers?

Bots run by sophisticated traders and firms. They monitor the mempool 24/7, identify profitable opportunities, and submit transactions with precisely calibrated gas prices — often within milliseconds of detecting a target transaction.

⛏️ Who Are the Validators?

Block producers who actually include transactions. Searchers pay them via high gas fees (or direct "tips" via MEV-Boost) to get their sandwiching/front-running transactions included in the right position.

02 / 07 — The Mempool

The Public Waiting Room

Before a transaction is confirmed in a block, it lives in the mempool — a publicly visible queue of pending transactions. This transparency is what makes MEV possible.

📋 What is the Mempool?

Every node on the network keeps a local copy of the memory pool — a list of valid, unconfirmed transactions waiting to be included in the next block. Since it's public, anyone can see every pending transaction, including what assets someone is trying to buy, at what price, and with what slippage tolerance.

The mempool is like a glass-walled trading floor. Everyone outside can see exactly what orders are being placed before they're executed. In traditional finance, this information would be strictly protected. In a public blockchain, it's visible to the entire world — including sophisticated bots looking for an edge.

🔍 What Does a Bot Actually See?

When Alice submits a transaction to swap 10 ETH for USDC on Uniswap, the bot sees: the exact function call, the amount in, the minimum amount out (slippage tolerance), the target contract address, the gas price. From slippage tolerance alone, the bot can calculate exactly how much it can exploit Alice's trade.

03 / 07 — Attack Type 1

Front-Running

The simplest and oldest MEV attack. A bot sees a profitable pending transaction and races to execute the same opportunity first by paying a higher gas fee.

🏃 How Front-Running Works

Alice discovers an NFT minting opportunity at a below-market price — 1 ETH for an NFT worth 3 ETH. She submits a transaction. A bot monitoring the mempool sees Alice's transaction, recognizes the profit, and immediately submits the same transaction with a higher gas price. The validator includes the higher-gas transaction first. The bot mints the NFT; Alice's transaction fails (the NFT is already minted).

In traditional finance, front-running by brokers is illegal under securities law — it's a breach of fiduciary duty. In blockchain, it's a structural feature of public mempools. No law is broken; the system design permits it. This is why many see it as an inherent flaw in public blockchains.

04 / 07 — Attack Type 2

The Sandwich Attack

The most common MEV attack targeting DEX traders. The attacker wraps the victim's swap in two transactions — buying before and selling after — guaranteeing a profit at the victim's expense.

🥪 The Mechanics

Bob wants to swap 10 ETH for USDC on Uniswap. The bot sees his pending transaction. It knows Bob set a 1% slippage tolerance. So the bot:

  • Front-run: Bot buys ETH with USDC before Bob — this raises ETH's price in the pool
  • Victim executes: Bob's swap goes through at the now-higher price (within his slippage tolerance)
  • Back-run: Bot immediately sells the ETH it just bought — now at the higher price Bob helped create
  • Bot pockets the difference. Bob got a worse rate than necessary.

📊 Scale of Sandwich Attacks

On busy days, sandwich bots extract millions of dollars from DEX traders. The average victim loses 0.1%–2% of their swap value to MEV. For small traders, this is often more costly than the gas fees themselves. Uniswap estimates MEV costs users hundreds of millions of dollars per year.

05 / 07 — Attack Type 3

MEV Arbitrage

Not all MEV is harmful. Arbitrage — exploiting price differences between exchanges — actually improves market efficiency. But it's still extractive for the traders who created those price differences.

⚖️ How DEX Arbitrage Works

When a large trade on Uniswap pushes ETH's price to $2,050 while it's still $2,000 on SushiSwap, arbitrage bots instantly buy ETH on SushiSwap and sell on Uniswap — making a risk-free profit and equalizing prices across venues. This is economically beneficial — it keeps prices aligned. But the bots take the profit rather than the original trader who moved the price.

Think of international currency traders who exploit exchange rate discrepancies between banks in different cities. When one bank quotes USD/EUR at 1.08 and another at 1.085, traders profit from the difference, and in doing so, force prices back into alignment. Blockchain arbitrage works identically — except it all happens in milliseconds within a single block.

✅ "Good" MEV

Pure arbitrage between DEXes brings prices into alignment, reducing information asymmetry and making markets more efficient. The value is extracted mostly from price inefficiency, not individual users.

❌ "Bad" MEV

Sandwich attacks and front-running directly extract value from individual users — giving them worse prices than they should have received. This is a regressive "invisible tax" that harms ordinary users most.

06 / 07 — Solutions

Fighting Back Against MEV

The Ethereum ecosystem has developed a range of technical and market-based solutions to reduce harmful MEV — though none eliminate it entirely.

🤫 Private Mempools (MEV Blocker / Flashbots Protect)

Instead of broadcasting your transaction to the public mempool, you send it directly to a validator via a private channel. Bots can't see it, so they can't front-run or sandwich it. Services like Flashbots Protect and MEV Blocker do this automatically when you use them as your RPC endpoint in MetaMask.

Used todayFree to users

📦 MEV-Boost & Flashbots

Rather than eliminating MEV, Flashbots democratizes and makes it transparent. Searchers submit "bundles" of transactions to a centralized relay (Flashbots). Validators receive these bundles and include the most profitable ones. Profits are shared between searchers and validators. This reduces chaotic "gas wars" and prevents the worst forms of blockchain congestion from MEV competition.

Dominant infrastructure~90% of Ethereum blocks

🔁 Commit-Reveal Schemes

Users submit a cryptographic hash of their transaction first (the "commit"), then reveal the actual transaction in the next block. Since bots can only see the hash — not the content — they can't act on it. Tradeoff: adds latency (two transactions required) and complexity.

Cryptographic protection

🕐 Time-Weighted Average Price (TWAP)

Use the average price over many blocks rather than the instantaneous price. Since a bot would need to manipulate the price across many blocks to move a TWAP — and that would be astronomically expensive — TWAP-based protocols are much more MEV-resistant for oracle attacks.

DeFi standard

🔀 Fair Ordering (Chainlink FSS)

A decentralized ordering system where transactions are ordered by their arrival time at the network — not by gas fee. Validators lose the ability to reorder transactions for profit. Still largely experimental, but promising for eliminating ordering-based MEV entirely.

Experimental

🔒 Slippage Protection (as a user)

Set your slippage tolerance as low as possible when swapping on DEXes. A 0.1% slippage gives sandwichers almost no room to profit — they can't squeeze any value within such a tight band. The tradeoff: your transaction might fail during volatile markets.

Actionable now

⚠️ The Fundamental Tension

MEV is ultimately caused by the combination of public mempools + miner/validator control over transaction ordering. Solving it fully means sacrificing either transparency (private mempools) or validator freedom (mandatory ordering rules) — both of which have trade-offs for decentralization and censorship resistance.
